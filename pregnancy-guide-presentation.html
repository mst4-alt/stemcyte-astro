Nutrition

Eating Well for Two

Daily servings your baby needs to grow. Most women gain 20–35 lbs during pregnancy.

Protein 3×/day
Chicken, fish, eggs, nuts, beans, soybeans
Calcium 3×/day
Milk, yogurt, cheese, leafy greens, almonds, sardines
Vegetables & Fruit 4×/day
Aim for variety and color
Fats & Oils 4×/day
1 serving = 1 tbsp oil, butter, or peanut butter
Grains 6×/day
Bread, cereal, rice, pasta

Eating by Trimester

1st Trimester
Your baby needs very little. Focus on what you can keep down — crackers, fruit, cheese, yogurt. Prenatal vitamin daily. At least 6 glasses of water.
2nd Trimester
Balanced diet becomes critical. Your baby is growing rapidly and needs the full range of nutrients. Follow the daily framework above.
3rd Trimester
Appetite may decrease even as baby grows fastest. 5–6 smaller meals daily. Avoid carbonated drinks. Good sources: eggs, beans, nuts, yogurt, leafy greens.

Know the Signs

When to Get Help

Your body will tell you when something needs attention. Don't wait.

1

Call Your Provider

  • Any vaginal bleeding or spotting
  • Fluid leaking from your vagina
  • Severe headache or changes in vision
  • Severe abdominal or pelvic pain
  • Fever over 100°F
  • Vomiting or diarrhea lasting 12+ hours
  • Noticeable decrease in fetal movement
  • Contractions 3–4×/hr before week 37
2

Go to the Hospital

  • Contractions every 5 minutes
  • Your water has broken
  • Vaginal bleeding (more than spotting)
  • Significant decrease in baby's movement
  • Fever over 100°F with other symptoms

Save your provider's number and hospital number in your phone now. Post them where your family can find them.

Safety

Avoid & Limit

Avoid

  • Smoking, alcohol, and recreational drugs — all increase risk of miscarriage, premature birth, and developmental problems. If you need help stopping, talk to your provider.
  • High-mercury fish (shark, swordfish, king mackerel, tilefish)
  • Raw meat, fish, or shellfish (sashimi, ceviche, raw oysters)
  • Soft cheeses (brie, feta, blue, goat, camembert)
  • Unpasteurized juice or milk
  • Cat litter — toxoplasma risk. Your cat is fine; the litter box isn't.
  • Hot tubs and saunas — risk of overheating and dehydration
  • Aspirin, Motrin, Ibuprofen, Aleve, Advil — may cause bleeding or complications

Limit

  • Caffeine: ≤200mg/day (about two 8oz cups)
  • All fish including tuna: under 12oz/week
  • Deli meats: heat until steaming before eating
  • Artificial sweeteners: occasional use is considered safe

Safe

  • Tylenol (acetaminophen) for pain
  • Warm baths (not hot tubs)
  • Cooked shellfish
  • Pasteurized dairy and juice
  • Gardening with gloves

Welcome

Congratulations.

You're pregnant. From this point forward, your body will do something extraordinary — growing a new life from a single cell. Some days you'll feel amazing. Others, you'll wonder what happened to your energy, your appetite, or your ability to stay awake past 8 PM.

All of it is normal. This guide walks you through what to expect, what to do about it, and what decisions are ahead — trimester by trimester.

1st Trimester

Your Body & Baby

Weeks 1–12

What You May Experience

Fatigue

Your body is building a placenta, increasing blood volume, and supporting rapid cell division. That takes enormous energy.

Rest when you can — don't fight it. Naps aren't laziness, they're biology.

Nausea

Rising hormone levels affect your digestive system. It can hit any time of day, not just mornings.

Dry crackers before getting up. Small bites every 30 min. Ginger tea or chews. Prenatal vitamin at bedtime.

Tender Breasts

Hormonal changes can make your breasts swollen and sensitive — sometimes seemingly overnight.

A well-fitted, supportive bra makes a real difference.

Bloating & Constipation

Your digestive system slows down to absorb more nutrients for the baby.

Increase fluids, fresh fruits, and vegetables. Chewable papaya enzyme tablets after meals can ease gas.

Cramping

Mild, menstrual-like cramps are normal as your uterus expands.

Sharp or severe pain is not normal — call your provider.

1st Trimester

Your To-Do List

Weeks 1–12

Complete your prenatal lab work — Blood type, Rh status, infection screening, and baseline health markers.

Discuss genetic testing with your provider — Several options exist at different stages. Understand what's available, what insurance covers, and what the results can and can't tell you.

Ask about preterm birth risk — About 1 in 10 babies are born early. Know your risk factors, the warning signs, and the plan if it happens.

Discuss blood pressure monitoring — High blood pressure in pregnancy can lead to pre-eclampsia. Early monitoring makes a difference.

Call your insurance company — Review what's covered for prenatal care, delivery, and newborn care before you need it.

Prioritize rest — Your body is working harder than it feels. Sleep when you're tired. It's not laziness — it's biology.

Start or continue exercising — Even 10 minutes of walking a day helps with fatigue, mood, and sleep quality.

Learn about cord blood preservation — Your baby's umbilical cord contains stem cells used in transplant medicine for over 30 years. You'll need to decide before delivery. See Panel 14.

Call Your Provider
  • Bleeding or severe abdominal pain
  • Severe headache or fever over 100°F
  • Vomiting or diarrhea lasting 12+ hours

02
2nd Trimester

Your Body & Baby

Weeks 12–28

What You May Experience

More Energy

Better sleep, returning appetite, and a noticeable lift in how you feel. This is often the most comfortable stretch of pregnancy.

Take advantage of this window — sign up for classes, prepare the nursery, enjoy how you feel.

Baby's Movement

Most women start feeling movement around week 23 — beginning as flutters and building to distinct kicks and rolls.

One of the most exciting milestones. By week 28, you'll begin tracking kick counts.

Varicose Veins

Increased blood volume and uterine pressure can cause veins in your legs to swell.

Wear support stockings. Elevate your legs when possible. Avoid crossing them.

Braxton Hicks Contractions

Mild uterine tightening 2–4 times a day is your body practicing for labor. Normal and not dangerous.

If contractions come 3–4 times in one hour, call your provider.

Anemia

If diagnosed, your iron levels need support. This is common and treatable.

Eat more meat, leafy greens, and beans. Take iron with juice, not milk. Increase fiber to offset constipation.

02
2nd Trimester

Your To-Do List

Weeks 12–28

AFP/Quad screen — A blood test between weeks 15–20 that screens for genetic disorders and neural tube defects. Most accurate at 16–18 weeks.

Level 2 ultrasound — Between weeks 18–22. A detailed evaluation of your baby's anatomy — brain, heart, limbs, and kidneys.

Start sleeping on your side — Beginning at week 20. Right or left side is fine — the goal is staying off your back. A body pillow can help.

Glucose screening — Between weeks 24–28. A one-hour blood test that screens for gestational diabetes.

RhoGAM injection — At week 28 if you are Rh-negative. This protects your current and future pregnancies.

Begin kick counts — Starting at week 28. When resting, you should feel at least 4 movements in one hour. Talk to your provider about their specific guidelines.

Decide on your labor support — Partner, family member, friend, or doula. Think about who you want in the room.

Sign up for childbirth and newborn care classes — Prepared childbirth, breastfeeding, and infant CPR. These fill up — register early.

Research cord blood preservation — Your baby's umbilical cord blood contains stem cells that have been used to treat over 80 serious conditions. It's worth understanding your options before delivery. See Panel 14.

Call Your Provider
  • Bleeding, leaking fluid, or changes in vision
  • Noticeable decrease in fetal movement
  • Pressure or pain 3–4 times per hour

3rd Trimester

Your Body & Baby

Weeks 28–Delivery

What You May Experience

Heartburn

Your baby pushes your stomach upward, causing acid reflux after meals or when lying flat.

Smaller meals, more often. Stay upright 30 min after eating. Lie on your right side. Antacids are safe.

Constipation & Hemorrhoids

Digestive slowdown and pelvic pressure make both common.

More fruits, vegetables, and water. Ask about a stool softener. OTC hemorrhoid remedies are available.

Back Pain & Leg Cramps

Extra weight shifts your center of gravity. Cramps tend to strike at night.

Bend at knees, not waist. For cramps, point toes toward belly and try ice. Prenatal massage and PT can help.

Swelling & Leaking

Fluid retention causes swollen feet/ankles. Bladder pressure can cause leaking when you laugh or cough.

Elevate legs. Increase water and protein. Kegels (50–100/day) help with leaking. Sudden severe swelling — call your provider.

Fatigue & Mood Changes

Interrupted sleep takes a toll on energy and emotions.

If you feel overwhelmed, anxious, or persistently sad, talk to your provider. Common and treatable.

3rd Trimester

Your To-Do List

Weeks 28–Delivery

Group B Strep screening — A vaginal and rectal swab between weeks 35–37. If positive, you'll receive antibiotics during labor to protect your baby.

Choose a pediatrician — Interview candidates and confirm they accept your insurance. Do this before your due date.

Take a hospital tour — Know the facility, where to park, and the fastest route from home.

Install the car seat — You will not be allowed to leave the hospital without one properly installed.

Pack your hospital bag — For you and for baby. Include your insurance card, ID, phone charger, and going-home outfits.

Discuss expanded newborn screening — A panel of tests performed at birth that screens your baby for a range of serious but treatable conditions.

Finalize your birth preferences — Discuss pain management, labor positions, and who will be in the room with your provider and support team.

Confirm your cord blood decision — If you haven't enrolled yet, it's not too late — even days before delivery. Visit stemcyte.com or call 1-888-CORD-BLOOD.
